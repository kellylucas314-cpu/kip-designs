About Cloud Seeding

Precipitation is not entirely accidental.

Cloud seeding is a form of weather modification that increases precipitation by introducing silver iodide particles into clouds during storm conditions. The particles give supercooled water droplets something to crystallize around — accelerating the natural formation of snowflakes and rain that would otherwise not fall.

The technology has been in use for over 70 years, with a proven 40-year track record in Santa Barbara County alone. SAWPA conducted a feasibility study in 2020 to assess potential benefits for the Santa Ana River watershed before launching the pilot program.

The watershed supplies water to more than 6 million residents across Los Angeles, Orange, San Bernardino, and Riverside counties.

"The practice of precipitation enhancement in Santa Barbara County has been used for over 40 years and has proven to be a cost effective and positive addition to our water resources management goals and objectives."

Matthew Scrudato, Senior Hydrologist — Santa Barbara County

The Process

Four steps from
cloud to snowpack.

When conditions are right — moist Pacific air, supercooled clouds — ground-based systems release silver iodide particles that trigger precipitation that might otherwise pass over the watershed without falling.

01

Storm Arrives

Pacific moisture flows over mountains, forming supercooled cloud decks

02

AgI Released

Silver iodide vaporized from ground stations, carried upward by wind

03

Crystals Form

Ice crystals nucleate on AgI particles; structure identical to ice

04

Snow Falls

Snowflakes grow heavy enough to fall, building snowpack through winter

01

Storms Arrive

Winter storms carry moist Pacific air over the mountains. Clouds form as air cools — composed of supercooled water droplets sitting just above freezing, primed to crystallize with the right trigger.

02

Seeding Begins

Ground stations vaporize silver iodide and acetone, releasing particles into the atmosphere. Orographic winds carry them upward into the cloud deck above the target mountain zones.

03

Ice Nucleates

Silver iodide has a crystal structure nearly identical to ice. Water vapor condenses and freezes onto particles — creating ice crystals in clouds where they wouldn't otherwise form naturally.

04

Snowpack Builds

Ice crystals grow as vapor deposits. Snowflakes accumulate mass and fall, building enhanced snowpack through winter that replenishes the watershed as temperatures rise in spring.

Ground-Based Equipment

Two delivery systems.
One mission.

Both systems release silver iodide into the atmosphere — but they're designed for different storm conditions. CNGs provide broad, sustained coverage; AHOGS deliver rapid, targeted bursts during convective events.

CNG

Cloud Nuclei Generators

Manually Operated · Continuous Burn

CNGs burn a solution of silver iodide and acetone to create a continuous plume of seeding material. Ideal for steady orographic lift conditions, where sustained broad coverage over mountain terrain is the priority.

  • Ideal for orographic lift — winds deflected upward by terrain
  • Creates a wide, continuous, persistent plume
  • Low installation and operating cost
  • 70+ year track record in operational programs worldwide
AHOGS

Automated High Output Ground Seeding

Remote-Controlled · Flare-Based

AHOGS systems use burn-in-place flares triggered remotely. Designed for convective storm dynamics — delivering a high-concentration burst of silver iodide precisely when rapidly changing conditions create a narrow window for effective seeding.

  • Designed for turbulent, convective storm events
  • Delivers higher silver iodide concentration per activation
  • No field personnel required — fully remote operation
  • Rapid deployment for short seeding windows

Environmental Safety

Decades of use.
Negligible risk.

Silver iodide has been studied as an environmental agent for over 70 years. The scientific consensus across independent research institutions, state agencies, and weather modification organizations is consistent and well-documented.

🌡️
Water in clouds can stay liquid down to 5°F — far below freezing — without a nucleating surface
❄️
Precipitation requires ice crystals to form, grow, and coalesce into snowflakes heavy enough to fall
🔬
Silver iodide's crystal structure is nearly identical to ice — making it a highly effective nucleating agent
🌱
Concentrations used in seeding programs are far below any environmentally meaningful threshold
💧
Natural nucleating agents include dust particles, pollen, and pollution — seeding mimics and enhances a natural process

Program Concluded · 2025

Validation Findings

What the science
actually found.

After a year of active seeding, SAWPA concluded the pilot program based on findings from the Desert Research Institute — an independent nonprofit research arm of the Nevada System of Higher Education contracted specifically for this validation.

Primary Finding

The validation report found little evidence that the cloud seeding program increased precipitation in the four target mountain zones. The target-and-control methodology — comparing seeded areas against unaffected control zones across multiple storm events — did not produce statistically significant precipitation enhancement.

Contributing Factor: Wildfire Burn Scars

Extensive wildfire burn scarring across the watershed introduced confounding variables into the study. Burned terrain behaves differently from intact forest during precipitation events, reducing the scientific reliability of the comparison methodology and contributing to the decision to end the program.

The Integrity of the Process

From the outset, SAWPA committed to letting the data decide. The decision to end the pilot program reflects exactly that commitment — rigorous, evidence-first water management for a region that cannot afford to invest in programs that don't deliver measurable results.
