About Cloud Seeding

Precipitation
is not
entirely accidental.

Cloud seeding is a form of weather modification that increases precipitation by introducing silver iodide particles into clouds during storm conditions. The particles give supercooled water droplets something to freeze around — accelerating the formation of snow and rain.

The technology has been in use for over 70 years. Southern California has a proven 40-year track record in Santa Barbara County alone.

"The practice of precipitation enhancement in Santa Barbara County has been used for over 40 years and has proven to be a cost effective and positive addition to our water resources management goals."

Matthew Scrudato, Senior Hydrologist — Santa Barbara County

The Process

Four steps from
cloud to snowpack.

01

Storms Arrive

Winter storms move into the watershed, carrying moist Pacific air over the mountains. Clouds form as the air cools, composed of supercooled water droplets sitting just above freezing — primed to crystallize.

02

Seeding Begins

Ground-based seeding stations vaporize a solution of silver iodide and acetone, releasing an invisible plume of particles into the atmosphere. Orographic winds carry them upward into the target cloud deck.

03

Ice Crystals Form

Silver iodide has a crystal structure nearly identical to ice. When particles enter the cold upper atmosphere, water vapor condenses and freezes onto them — creating ice crystals that wouldn't have formed otherwise.

04

Precipitation Falls

Ice crystals grow progressively larger as vapor continues to deposit. Snowflakes form, accumulate mass, and eventually fall. Enhanced snowpack accumulates through winter, replenishing the watershed as it melts in spring.

Targeted Regions

Four mountain
zones. One
watershed.

SAWPA analyzed decades of storm events and seasonal runoff data to identify four distinct mountain regions contributing most significantly to the Santa Ana River's flow. Each zone is served by ground-based seeding sites selected for maximum orographic effectiveness.

01
San Bernardino Mountains
Primary snowpack contributor for upper watershed flows
02
San Gabriel Mountains
Critical runoff zone for Los Angeles and San Bernardino basins
03
San Jacinto Mountains
Southeastern watershed contributor, Riverside County recharge
04
Santa Ana Mountains
Southern watershed boundary, Orange County groundwater recharge

Ground-Based Equipment

Two delivery systems.
One goal.

CNG

Cloud Nuclei Generators

Manually Operated · Continuous Burn

CNGs burn a solution of silver iodide and acetone, creating a continuous plume of seeding material that disperses broadly across mountainous terrain. Best suited for steady orographic conditions where consistent coverage over a large area is the priority.

  • Ideal for orographic lift — winds deflected by terrain
  • Creates a wide, continuous plume
  • Low installation and operating cost
  • Proven in decades of program use worldwide
AHOGS

Automated High Output Ground Seeding

Remote-Controlled · Flare-Based

AHOGS systems use burn-in-place flares triggered remotely, releasing a high concentration of silver iodide over a short, precise window. Designed for convective storm conditions — where turbulence and rapid dynamics require a targeted burst rather than a sustained plume.

  • Ideal for convective, turbulent storm events
  • Delivers higher silver iodide concentration
  • Rapid remote deployment with no field personnel required
  • Precise timing for dynamic storm windows

Environmental Safety

Decades of use.
Negligible risk.

Silver iodide has been studied extensively as an environmental agent. The scientific consensus across independent research, state agencies, and weather modification organizations is consistent: at concentrations used in cloud seeding programs, it poses no meaningful risk to human health, wildlife, or ecosystems.

"Cloud seeding is a safe and effective means of augmenting local water supplies."
California Department of Water Resources
"Research has clearly documented that cloud seeding with silver-iodide aerosols shows no environmentally harmful effect."
North American Weather Modification Council
"Water in a cloud deck can remain liquid until it reaches temperatures as low as 5°F — introducing a nucleating agent enhances the natural snowflake formation process."
Pacific Northwest National Laboratory

Pilot Program Validation

What the science
actually found.

Program Status — Concluded 2025

The pilot program has ended.

After a year of active seeding and validation analysis, SAWPA concluded the Santa Ana River Watershed Cloud Seeding Pilot Program. The decision was based on a formal validation report conducted by the Desert Research Institute (DRI), a nonprofit research arm of the Nevada System of Higher Education.

The report found little evidence that the program increased precipitation in the target areas. Additionally, the presence of wildfire burn scars throughout the region introduced confounding variables that affected the scientific reliability of the study.

This outcome reflects the rigorous, evidence-first approach SAWPA committed to at the program's outset — the goal was always to let the science decide.

Read the full validation report (PDF) →

The validation used a target-and-control methodology: comparing the four seeded mountain zones against unaffected control areas across multiple storm events. This independent approach, contracted through DRI, was designed to provide the most defensible scientific assessment possible.
